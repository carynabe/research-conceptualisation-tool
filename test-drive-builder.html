Test drive your research — builder

Work through each stage to discover the alignment in your research design before you build your presentation. The slides come after the thinking, not before.

What alignment means here: Structural and conceptual alignment is based on a set of judgements the researcher has intentionally negotiated. This flow represents your aligned project, where the research design supports answering the research question and the questions in turn speak directly to unpacking the research problem. Logical coherence is the justification of that flow — from conceptualisation, purpose, problem, questions, key literature and concepts, and the research gap, through to the research design.

Stage 1 — Foundation
Your PPSQ
Everything in your research design flows from and returns to the PPSQ. Before testing the logic of your design, the PPSQ itself needs to be clear and coherent. State it here — this is your anchor for every decision that follows.

RQ answered
Purpose State your purpose statement
One sentence. Format: "The purpose of this study is to [analytical verb] [what] in [context] in order to [contribution]."
Problem State your problem statement
Three to five sentences. Context → gap → tension → stakes. (No filler. Every sentence earns its place.)
Research questions State your research questions
Two to three questions. Each must follow from the problem statement. (Avoid: to what extent, evaluate, assess. Use: examine, analyse, investigate, trace, account for.)
Alignment check

Does the verb in your purpose statement match the kind of investigation your questions are asking for? (If the purpose says "examine" but the questions ask "to what extent," the logic is already broken.)

Stage 2 — Conceptual framework
The lens you are looking through
A conceptual framework is not a list of theories. It is the set of analytical tools — concepts, theoretical traditions, key arguments — that you are using to look at your problem. It determines what you can see and what you cannot. It must connect directly to your research questions.

RQ answered
Key concepts What are the two or three central concepts in your framework?
Name each concept and give it a meaning in context — not a dictionary definition. How does each concept help you see or analyse something specific about your research problem? (A concept without contextual meaning is just a label.)
Theoretical tradition What theoretical tradition grounds your framework?
Who developed the theoretical tools you are using? How has this tradition been applied in comparable studies? Where does your application align with or deviate from prior use? (If you cannot name a tradition, you have a topic, not a framework.)
Framework to RQ connection How does your framework connect to each research question?
For each research question, identify which concept or theoretical tool does the analytical work. (If a research question has no connection to your framework, either the question or the framework needs revision.)
Alignment check

Does your framework provide tools to answer your research questions — or does it describe background to your topic? (Background belongs in the literature review. The framework belongs in the analysis.)

Stage 3 — Methodology
Your philosophical and design choices
Methodology is not the same as methods. It is the philosophical position that justifies your approach — why qualitative, why document analysis, why mixed methods. It must follow logically from your research problem and questions. A methodology chosen because it seems manageable is not a justified methodology.

RQ answered
Approach Which methodological approach are you using?
Qualitative
Concerned with meaning, interpretation, and context. Suited to understanding how and why.
Document analysis
Systematic examination of existing texts, records, or artefacts as primary data sources.
Mixed methods
Combines qualitative and quantitative approaches. Requires explicit justification for the combination.
Philosophical position What assumptions are you making about the social reality you are studying?
In plain terms: are you treating your phenomenon as something that exists independently of interpretation, or as something constructed through it? (You do not need jargon, but you do need to be able to state your position.)
Justification Why is this methodology appropriate for your specific research problem?
Connect your methodology directly to your problem statement and research questions. Why would a different approach not work here? (Justification by elimination is valid. Saying why alternatives do not fit is as strong as saying why yours does.)
Alignment check

Does your methodology follow from your research questions — or did you choose it first and retrofit the questions? (A panel reviewer will ask this directly.)

Stage 4 — Methods and instruments
How you will collect and analyse
Methods are the specific techniques you will use to gather and analyse data. They must follow from your methodology. Each method needs a justification — not just a description of what it is, but why it is the right tool for this problem.

RQ answered
Data collection How will you gather your data?
Name your data collection method/s. For each: what will it produce, and which research question does it speak to? (For secondary data studies: where are the documents or datasets coming from, and what makes them appropriate sources?)
Analysis How will you analyse your data?
Name your analytical approach. Explain what it does to data and why that process produces the kind of knowledge your study needs. ("I will analyse the data thematically" is not enough. Explain what thematic analysis does and why that fits your questions.)
Instruments What instruments will you use, if any?
Describe any instruments — interview guides, coding frameworks, document review templates — and explain how they operationalise your research questions. (If your study uses secondary data only, explain how you will apply your analytical framework to the documents.)
Alignment check

Do your methods follow from your methodology — or were they chosen because they seem practical? (Convenience is not a methodological justification.)

Stage 5 — Data sources
What you are working with and why
Data sources are proxies. They stand in for concepts you cannot observe directly. The question is not just what data you have access to — it is what each source makes visible about your research problem, and what it cannot show.

RQ answered
Sources What are your data sources?
For each source: name it, explain how you will access it, identify which concept or RQ it operationalises, and state what it can and cannot show. (The "cannot show" is as important as the "can show." A source that appears to answer everything has not been thought through.)
Adequacy Are your data sources adequate to answer your research questions?
Take each research question: do your identified sources provide enough material to investigate it? Where are the gaps? (If a RQ cannot be answered by any identified source, you need either a different source or a different question.)
Alignment check

Are your data sources chosen because they are the best available proxies for your concepts — or because they were easy to find? (Name both, but be honest about the difference.)

Stage 6 — Ethical considerations
What your study owes to those it involves
Ethics in research is not a compliance checklist. It is a set of reasoned commitments about how your study will treat the people, institutions, and knowledge it engages with. Even secondary data studies carry ethical obligations — around attribution, representation, and the use of sensitive information.

RQ answered
Ethical dimensions What ethical considerations are relevant to your study?
Does your study involve human subjects directly or indirectly? Sensitive documents or personal data? Politically contested material? Vulnerable communities? ("This study uses secondary data so there are no ethical concerns" is rarely correct. Name the considerations even if you then argue they are minimal.)
Justification How have you addressed or will you address these considerations?
For each consideration: what steps have you taken or will you take? Where risks cannot be eliminated, how have you minimised or acknowledged them? (A well-justified ethical position names the risk and explains the mitigation — it does not deny the risk exists.)
Alignment check

Are your ethical considerations specific to your study — or generic statements that could apply to any research? (Generic ethics sections signal that the researcher has not thought through the specific risks of their own design.)

Weak links Where does your chain feel weakest?
Which link between elements is hardest to justify? Name it and explain what you would need to do to strengthen it before your presentation. (Naming a weak link is a sign of analytical rigour, not failure. Panels respect researchers who know where their design is under pressure.)
Panel question What is the hardest question a panel reviewer could ask you?
Anticipate the sharpest critique of your design. State it as a question. Then answer it. (If you cannot answer it, your presentation will not survive peer review. The purpose of this exercise is to find that out now, not in the room.)
