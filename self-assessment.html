Is your argument actually holding?

This is a diagnostic self-assessment. It sits between developing your research thinking and testing your research design. Its purpose is to expose logical failures before they get embedded in your methodology. Answer every question as precisely as you can. Vague answers are the diagnosis.

Before you start: These questions are deliberately difficult. They are not asking whether you have done the work — they are asking whether the work is sound. A student with genuine intellectual alignment will find these questions answerable, specific, and even satisfying to respond to. A student whose argument has gaps will find them uncomfortable. That discomfort is information. Use it before you move to Tool 2, not after.
1
Failure mode 1 — Misalignment
The chain is broken somewhere
Elements of your PPSQ and design do not connect to each other. The purpose verb does not match the methodology. Research questions introduce concepts not in the problem statement. Data sources do not speak to the questions.
Take your first research question. Now take your problem statement. In two sentences — not more — explain how answering that question would resolve the specific intellectual problem you stated. Do not paraphrase either. Make the logical connection explicit.
the connection does not exist, or you need more than two sentences to establish it, the question and the problem are not yet aligned.
Look at your purpose verb — investigate, examine, analyse, critically discuss. Now look at your proposed methodology. Do they belong to the same mode of inquiry? Name the verb, name the methodology, and justify in one sentence why they are compatible.
you chose the verb for style and the methodology for convenience, and they are pulling in different directions.
List every concept that appears in your research questions. Now check your problem statement — does each of those concepts appear there too, or are they being introduced for the first time in the questions? Concepts that appear in questions but not in the problem statement are not yet grounded.
you are introducing analytical machinery in the questions that the problem statement has not prepared the ground for.
Take each data source you have identified. For each one, name the specific research question it speaks to and explain what kind of evidence it produces that is relevant to that question. If a data source cannot be linked to a specific research question, it does not belong in your study.
you have data sources that feel relevant but do not connect to any specific question — which means the design has loose ends.
2
Failure mode 2 — Unconvincing argument
You are describing, not arguing
The problem statement describes a situation rather than arguing for an intellectual gap. The contribution claim is circular. The tension statement restates the AND rather than introducing genuine friction between ideas.
Read your problem statement aloud. At what point does it stop describing what is happening in the world and start arguing that something is not yet known or understood in the scholarship? If that transition does not happen, you do not have a problem statement — you have a situation report.
every sentence describes a social condition. You have written about the world, not about the state of knowledge about the world.
State your contribution claim in one sentence. Now remove the words "contribute," "understanding," "knowledge," and "field" from it. What is left? If nothing substantive remains, the claim is circular and needs to be rebuilt from the specific finding your study will produce.
the contribution claim evaporates when you remove the generic academic vocabulary — which means it was never a claim, just a placeholder.
Your problem statement should make a case that your study is necessary. Not useful, not interesting — necessary. Complete this sentence without using the word "important": "This study is necessary because without it, the field will continue to be unable to explain..." If you cannot complete it, the necessity of your study has not been established.
you can explain why the topic matters in the world but not why the study is intellectually necessary — which means you have a justification for caring, not for researching.
What is the single most vulnerable claim in your argument — the one a panel reviewer could most easily challenge? State the claim. Then state the strongest version of the challenge. Then explain how your study design addresses it.
you cannot identify a vulnerable claim, which means you have not stress-tested your argument — or worse, the entire argument is vulnerable and you have not noticed.
3
Failure mode 3 — False knowledge gap
The gap is in your reading, not in the literature
You have identified a gap in what you have read, not a gap in what exists. The gap disappears the moment someone points you to three obvious sources. You have not read enough to know what is known.
Name the specific claim you are making about the knowledge gap. Now name at least three scholars who have written about your topic area and explain precisely why their work does not fill the gap you have identified. Not why it is different from your topic — why it specifically does not resolve your stated intellectual problem.
you can name scholars in your area but cannot explain why their work leaves your specific gap open — which means you have not read them carefully enough to know what they actually argue.
Has your topic been studied in a comparable context — a different country, institution, sector, or time period? If yes: what did those studies find, and why do their findings not transfer to your context? If no: why has no comparable study been done, and what does that absence tell you about the state of the field?
you have not looked for comparable studies, which means the gap you have identified may already be filled elsewhere and you have not checked.
Is your knowledge gap a gap in empirical evidence, a gap in theoretical explanation, a gap in contextual application, or a gap in methodological approach? Name which type it is and explain why that classification matters for how your study is designed to fill it.
you cannot classify the type of gap, which means you have not been precise enough about what kind of knowledge your study will actually produce.
If your supervisor handed you three well-chosen sources tomorrow that you had not read — sources directly relevant to your topic, context, and questions — what is the worst case scenario for your gap claim? Would it survive? Would it need to be narrowed? Could it disappear entirely?
the gap would disappear with three more sources — which means it is not a gap in the literature, it is a gap in your reading.
4
Failure mode 4 — Undialectical literature engagement
You are accumulating sources, not building an argument
Sources are summarised not argued. AND-BUT-THEREFORE labels are applied mechanically rather than representing genuine intellectual movement. There is no tension between sources — only accumulation. The literature review describes what scholars have said rather than showing where they agree, where they conflict, and what that conflict opens up.
State your BUT in one sentence without using the word "however," "but," or "although." The BUT is the intellectual tension at the heart of your study — the point where the established literature fails to account for something your study investigates. If you need a connective word to create the tension, the tension is not yet in the ideas themselves.
you are using grammatical contrast to simulate intellectual tension. The tension needs to be in the substance of what the sources claim, not in how you connect them.
Pick any two of your sources — one AND and one BUT. Do not summarise either. Instead, stage a confrontation between them: what specific claim does the AND source make that the BUT source directly challenges or complicates, and what does that confrontation reveal about the state of knowledge on your topic?
you can summarise both sources but cannot make them speak to each other — which means you are treating them as independent items in a list rather than as participants in an intellectual conversation.
What would a scholar who disagreed with your THEREFORE say? Name the strongest opposing position — not a straw man, but a genuinely held alternative view in the literature. Then explain why your study is positioned the way it is rather than adopting that opposing view.
there is no opposing view in your framework — which means you have not engaged with the full range of scholarly positions, only those that support the direction you had already decided on.
Your THEREFORE sources are supposed to point toward productive investigation of the tension you identified. For each THEREFORE source, explain: what specifically does it offer that neither the AND nor the BUT sources provide? If a THEREFORE source could be moved to AND or BUT without any loss, it is not doing THEREFORE work.
your THEREFORE sources are just more AND sources — they agree with and extend the foundation without providing the tools or direction to investigate the tension.
5
The golden thread test
Does a single coherent intellectual argument run through the whole study?
A well-aligned proposal has a golden thread — one central intellectual argument that runs visibly from the problem statement through the research questions, through the literature engagement, through the conceptual framework, and into the research design. Every element should be a manifestation of that central argument. If you pull on any element, the whole thing should move.
State your central intellectual argument in three sentences. Not your topic. Not your research questions. The argument — the claim your study is making about why a particular phenomenon, in a particular context, requires investigation through a particular lens. If you need more than three sentences, the argument is not yet clear enough to anchor a proposal.
you can describe your study but cannot state its central argument — which means there is not yet a golden thread, only a collection of related interests.
Take your central argument and trace it through each element of your proposal: purpose statement, problem statement, each research question, your conceptual framework, your methodology. At each point, explain in one phrase how that element expresses or serves the central argument. If any element cannot be connected back, it is either redundant or misaligned.
some elements of your proposal are doing their own thing — they exist because they seem necessary for a proposal, not because they serve the argument.
If your study produces exactly the findings you expect — if everything goes as designed — what will the field be able to say that it cannot say now? State this as a single declarative sentence in the present tense, as if the study is complete. This is your contribution. If you cannot state it, you do not yet know what your study is for.
you can describe the process of the study but not its intellectual destination — which means the study does not yet have a clear purpose beyond producing a document.
