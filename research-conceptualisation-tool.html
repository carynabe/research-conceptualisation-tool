Masters research thinking tool

Work through each stage in order. The PPSQ is written last — after the thinking is done.

1. Provocation
2. Who says so
3. AND
4. BUT
5. THEREFORE
6. PPSQ
Module 1 — Problem constitution
The social provocation
You are not writing yet. You are thinking out loud about a situation that concerns you. The task here is to establish whether this is a researchable intellectual problem — not just a social issue worth caring about.
Situation What is actually happening, and where?
Describe the situation in plain language — not academic prose. What is occurring, in what context, and what makes it visible as a problem? (Write this as you would explain it to a non-expert. Avoid policy language at this stage.)
Problem constitution Who says this is a problem — and from what position?
Not everyone agrees on what constitutes a problem, or why. Identify who has named this as a problem: is it government, civil society, academics, affected communities, international bodies? Do they agree on what the problem is, or do they frame it differently? (This establishes that your problem has a social location — it did not arise from nowhere.)
Nature of the problem What kind of problem is this?
Choose the most accurate description and explain why it fits. Is this primarily: a political problem (about power, interests, contestation), a systems failure (about institutional design, capacity, or dysfunction), or a genuinely debated problem in the literature (where scholars disagree about causes, mechanisms, or solutions)? (You may find it is more than one. Name the dominant framing and explain why you are foregrounding it.)

Module 1 — Problem constitution continued
Scholars who have identified this problem
Before engaging the broader literature, identify three to four scholars who have specifically recognised and investigated the problem you are describing. This grounds your problem statement in the academic conversation rather than in your own observation alone.
Problem sources Who else has seen and investigated this problem?
Identify three to four sources. For each: name the author and year, state what problem they identified, and briefly describe how they investigated it — what context, what approach, what data or evidence. (You are not summarising their findings yet. You are establishing that this problem has a scholarly history.)
Agreement Where do these scholars agree?
What do these sources share in common — in how they define the problem, what they take as causes, or what they identify as at stake? (Agreement establishes the intellectual ground. It shows there is something solid to build from.)
Generative differences Where do their approaches differ — and why does that matter?
Identify differences that are intellectually productive — where scholars use different frameworks, study different contexts, or reach different conclusions. Why are these differences generative rather than just contradictory? (Generative differences are the seed of your BUT sources. They signal that the problem is genuinely unresolved.)
Module 2 — Dialectical reading
AND: the foundational literature
AND sources establish the intellectual ground on which your argument stands — what the field knows, broadly agrees on, and takes as the starting point for further inquiry. You are not agreeing uncritically. You are building from them.
AND

What is established. The ground your argument stands on.

BUT

Where that ground gives way. The tension that makes inquiry necessary.

THEREFORE

What direction the tension opens. What your study can do.

AND What does the established literature agree on?
Identify three to four AND sources. For each: what foundational claim does this source make? How does it support the intellectual case for your study? (Read for argument, not summary. What does this source establish that your study depends on?)
AND — proxies How did these sources investigate their claims?
For each AND source: what data, indicators, or measures did the authors use? How did they operationalise abstract concepts into investigable phenomena? (A concept like "institutional accountability" cannot be measured directly. What did the authors use as a proxy, and what does that choice reveal about their assumptions?)
Module 2 — Dialectical reading
BUT: where the literature breaks down
BUT sources are the engine of your argument. Without genuine intellectual tension there is no case for your study. BUT sources do not simply disagree — they reveal where the foundational literature fails to account for something important.
BUT Where does the established literature fall short?
Identify three to four BUT sources. For each: what does this source reveal that the AND sources cannot account for? Does it challenge a foundational assumption, expose a gap in the evidence, show the framework does not travel to a different context, or demonstrate that findings do not hold under different conditions? (The BUT is where you demonstrate that your study is necessary.)
Tension statement State the core tension in two sentences.
If you cannot compress the AND/BUT tension into two sentences, the dialectical structure is not yet clear enough to write a problem statement. Format: "The literature establishes [X]. However, [Y] demonstrates that this does not hold when [condition], which means the field currently cannot explain [Z]."
Scholarly positioning Where is your study positioned in this debate?
Name at least two scholarly positions on this tension. Then explain where your study sits — are you resolving the tension, investigating it empirically, applying one side to a new context, or proposing a different framing? (Your position is a reasoned intellectual stance grounded in the literature, not a personal opinion.)
Module 2 — Dialectical reading
THEREFORE: what your study can contribute
THEREFORE is not a conclusion — it is a direction. These sources show what productive investigation of your tension looks like, and what kind of new knowledge your study can generate.
THEREFORE What sources point toward productive investigation?
Identify two to four THEREFORE sources. These might offer: a theoretical framework with tools for navigating the tension, empirical evidence from comparable contexts, or methodological approaches that open the question differently. (THEREFORE sources do not have to solve the problem. They have to show that investigating it is worthwhile and feasible.)
Contribution claim What will the field know after your study that it does not know now?
State your contribution claim precisely. This is not what you will do — it is what the field will gain. (Avoid: "This study will contribute to understanding of X." Try instead: "This study will show whether [claim] holds in [context], and under what conditions — which the existing literature cannot yet account for.")
Data sources What secondary data will you use, and how does each connect to your questions?
For each data source: name it, explain how you will access it, and identify what concept or research question it operationalises. (Data sources are proxies. Explain HOW each source stands in for a concept — what it makes visible and what it cannot show.)
Module 3 — Synthesis
Writing the PPSQ
Note on order: The PPSQ is read first by your supervisor, but it is written last by you. What you write here is a crystallisation of thinking you have already done — not a guess at what sounds academic. It should feel achievable now.
Purpose verb Choose your analytical verb deliberately
The verb in your purpose statement does methodological work. It signals your epistemological stance and commits you to a mode of inquiry. Choose one and be prepared to justify it to your supervisor.
Investigate
Implies empirical inquiry — uncovering evidence about something not yet fully documented.
Commits to: evidence-finding, empirical orientation
Examine
Implies applying a lens or framework. The thing exists; you are looking at it through a specific instrument.
Commits to: a defined theoretical frame applied to a case
Analyse
Implies decomposition — taking something apart to understand its components or relationships.
Commits to: a defined object and an explicit method of disaggregation
Critically discuss
Implies engaging competing positions and arriving at a reasoned judgement. Suits conceptual studies.
Commits to: taking a position, not just reporting findings
Explore
Commits to nothing. Signals uncertainty about what kind of knowledge the study will produce.
Avoid at Masters level
Assess / Evaluate
Implies criteria-based judgement against a standard. Specific to audit and evaluation methodology.
Only if your method is explicitly evaluative
Purpose statement One sentence.
Format: "The purpose of this study is to [analytical verb] [what] in [context] in order to [contribution]."
Problem statement Three to five sentences. No filler.
Draw directly on the thinking you have done: the social provocation, the scholarly framing, the AND/BUT tension, and the knowledge gap. Every sentence earns its place. (Structure: context → what the literature establishes → where it falls short → what therefore remains unknown → why this matters.)
Research questions Two to three questions only.
Use these verbsexamine · analyse · investigate · trace · account for · understand · describe · explain
Avoid theseto what extent · evaluate · assess · determine the impact · measure the effectiveness
For each question run this check: (a) Does it address the gap in the problem statement? (b) Is it investigable with your identified data sources? (c) Would answering it produce your stated contribution? (If any answer is no, revise the question — not the check.)
